How It Works

Reference covering the methodology, data sources, and math behind every number that is displayed. Understanding the calculations help use the tool more effectively and spot where situations might differ from the defaults explained below.

Philosophy

Transparent Math Federal tax, state tax, FICA, budget ranges are calculated from its source formula with no black boxes. The data files that store these figures, (tax_rates.json, taxes.py), are plain text and readable.

Frameworks and Recommendation Sources Budget percentages, emergency fund targets, and retirement milestones are sourced from CFPB, Fidelity, and peer-reviewed personal finance frameworks. Every recommendation cites its source.

Conservative Bias When guidance gives a range (e.g. "save 10 - 15%"), it defaults to displaying the full range rather than the minimum.

Estimates, not Guarantees It models a standardized taxpayer taking the standard deduction with no special credits, additional income sources, or itemized deductions. It is a planning tool, not a substitute for a CPA or CFP.

Tax Calculations

Federal Income Tax

Federal income tax is progressive, with each bracket only applying to income within that bracket, not the total income. The standard deduction is subtracted from gross income first to produce taxable income, and the brackets are applied to that.

taxable_income = gross_income - standard_deduction federal_tax = Σ (each_bracket_amount x marginal_rate) effective_rate = federal_tax ÷ gross_income

Example — $100,000 gross, Single filer, 2025:

Standard deduction: $15,000 Taxable income: $85,000 10% on $0 → $11,925 = $1,192.50 12% on $11,925 → $48,475 = $4,386.00 22% on $48,475 → $85,000 = $8,035.50 ─────────── Federal tax: $13,614.00 Effective rate: 13.61% Marginal rate: 22%

IRS Rev. Proc. 2024-40   Bracket thresholds are inflation-adjusted annually. The tool auto-selects the most recent available year ≤ the current calendar year.

FICA (Social Security & Medicare)

FICA is separate from income tax and not affected by the standard deduction; it applies to the gross wages directly.

Social Security: 6.20% of wages up to $176,100 (2025 wage base) Medicare: 1.45% of all wages (no cap) Additional Medicare: 0.90% of wages over $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ) FICA total = SS + Medicare + Additional Medicare

The SS wage base is adjusted annually for wage inflation and announced by the SSA in October. Employers match the 6.2% SS and 1.45% Medicare contributions; self-employed individuals pay the combined 15.3% (see Limitations).

IRS Publication 15 (Circular E)   SSA COLA Notice

State Income Tax

States fall into three categories, each calculated differently:

Type States Calculation
No tax AK, FL, NV, NH, SD, TN, TX, WA, WY State tax = $0
Flat rate AZ (2.5%), CO (4.4%), IL (4.95%), PA (3.07%), and others (gross - state_deduction - exemptions) x flat_rate
Progressive CA, NY, MN, NJ, OR, and others Same bracket logic as federal, using state-specific thresholds and deductions

Each state has its own standard deduction and personal exemption amounts (often different from the federal figures). States currently phasing their rates down (NC, GA, IA, IN, MO, MS, SC) have year-specific overrides applied automatically.

"Under" isn't always good as spending significantly under on healthcare or insurance may indicate underinsurance rather than frugality.

Budget Frameworks

The framework comparison table shows four established approaches applied to the given gross monthly income:

50/30/20 — Elizabeth Warren
50% needs · 30% wants · 20% savings

Popularized in All Your Worth: The Ultimate Lifetime Money Plan (2005) by Senator Elizabeth Warren and Amelia Warren Tyagi. Designed as a flexible, sustainable framework for most Americans. A widely cited personal budgeting rule.

Dave Ramsey — Baby Steps
55% needs · 20% wants · 25% savings/debt

From The Total Money Makeover and Ramsey Solutions. More conservative on lifestyle, more aggressive on debt elimination and wealth building. Allocates more to needs (reflecting real world cost of living) and less to discretionary wants.

FIRE (Lean) — Financial Independence
40% needs · 10% wants · 50% savings

Popularized by Mr. Money Mustache and Early Retirement Extreme (Jacob Lund Fisker). Targets a savings rate of 50%+ to achieve financial independence and optional early retirement within 15-20 years. Requires significant lifestyle optimization. Best suited to high earners with low fixed costs.

70/20/10 — High Cost-of-Living
70% needs · 10% giving/fun · 20% savings

Adapted from various sources for lower income households or very high cost of living markets (NYC, SF, Boston) where 70% of gross may legitimately go to essential living costs. The 10% "wants" allocation covers giving, charity, or modest discretionary spending which reflects the Dave Ramsey tithe guidance and the reality that there's little room for pure lifestyle spending at this budget level.

Note: No single framework fits everyone. Use the comparison table to see which framework most closely matches actual spending pattern, then use that as a planning target.

Financial Health Check

Enter account balances (checking, savings, retirement) to unlock the health check section. All three fields are optional; enter only what is applicapble.

Emergency Fund Coverage

liquid_total = checking_balance + savings_balance monthly_expenses = entered_actual_spending (or 80% of net if no actuals entered) months_liquid = liquid_total ÷ monthly_expenses
  • Under 1 month: Critical — one unexpected expense becomes debt
  • 1-3 months: Warning — minimum met, build toward 3+
  • 3-6 months: On track — CFPB recommended minimum
  • 6+ months: Fully funded — consider investing the excess

Net Worth Estimate

net_worth_estimate = checking + savings + retirement_balance

This is a simplified estimate that excludes liabilities (mortgage, student loans, credit card debt), home equity, business equity, and non-retirement investment accounts. It reflects liquid + retirement assets only, labeled clearly as an estimate.

Known Limitations

This tool models a simplified, standardized taxpayer. Actual tax liability will differ if any of the following apply.
  • ⚠Standard deduction only. Itemized deductions (mortgage interest, charitable contributions, state/local taxes up to $10k SALT cap) are not modeled. If itemized, federal taxable income is lower and actual tax is less than shown.
  • ⚠No tax credits. The child tax credit, earned income tax credit (EITC), education credits, EV credits, etc. can substantially reduce taxes owed and are not included here.
  • ⚠No local taxes. City and county income taxes are excluded — notably NYC (3.08-3.88%), Yonkers (1.48%), Philadelphia (3.75%), Baltimore (2.25%), and Ohio municipal taxes (1-2.5%).
  • ⚠No self-employment tax. Freelancers and 1099 contractors pay both the employee and employer halves of FICA (15.3% combined) rather than the 7.65% modeled here. The SE deduction is also not included.
  • ⚠No capital gains. Long term capital gains (0%, 15%, 20%) and qualified dividends are taxed differently from ordinary income and are not modeled.
  • ⚠No AMT. The Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) can apply to high-income filers with certain deductions and is not modeled.
  • ⚠Pre-tax benefits not modeled. 401(k) contributions, HSA contributions, FSA, and employer-paid health premiums reduce federal (and often state) taxable income. Entering gross salary without accounting for these makes modeled tax slightly higher than actual.
  • ⚠State rates may lag mid-transition. Seven states are currently phasing their rates down annually (NC, GA, IN, IA, MO, MS, SC). The 2025 override values are current; post-2025 phase-down steps will need a new override entry each year.
  • ⚠Single income modeled. Two-income households (Married Filing Jointly) should enter combined gross income. This may produce a bracket estimate that differs from running each income separately through single filer brackets ("marriage penalty/bonus" effects are not modeled in detail).
  • ⚠Retirement balance excludes liabilities. The net worth estimate excludes home equity (home value minus mortgage), car equity, investment accounts outside retirement, and all liabilities.

Tips for Best Results

Use W-2 for income

Use Box 1 (wages) from W-2 rather than the stated salary. Box 1 already subtracts pre-tax 401(k) and HSA contributions, so the tax calculation will be more accurate than using gross salary figure.

Update every January

After receiving W-2 and after major income changes (raise, new job, bonus), re-run the calculator. The new tax year data is applied automatically; no action needed beyond entering your updated income.

Enter actuals for real insights

The insights engine is only as useful as actual spending data. Open the "Current spending" section and enter real monthly amounts from bank or credit card statements, even rough estimates produce meaningful insights.

Include all retirement accounts

The retirement balance field should be the total across all accounts: 401(k), 403(b), IRA, Roth IRA, SEP-IRA, etc. Find totals from plan provider's website or via an aggregator like Fidelity Full View.

Re-run after life events

Major life changes that affect taxes or budget include: marriage, divorce, having a child, buying a home, starting a business, or changing states. Each should prompt a re-run with updated inputs.

Use it for scenario planning

Try different income levels to see the tax impact of a raise or a new job offer. Enter a higher retirement contribution to see how it affects take-home. Adjust housing costs to evaluate a move decision.

Consult a professional for complex situations. This tool is designed for planning. For situations involving business income, significant investments, inheritance, complex deductions, or major tax events, consult a Certified Public Accountant (CPA) or Certified Financial Planner (CFP).
